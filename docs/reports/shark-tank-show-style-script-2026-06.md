## Opening Position

This version should feel bolder than the original audience deck. The story is not "look at this tool." The story is:

> Agent adoption is moving fast. I have proven a readiness pattern that helps teams build useful agents faster while keeping ownership, evidence, measurement, and human approval clear.

Use this version when you want the room to feel the business hook quickly, then walk them through the product, proof, market, ask, guardrails, and deal.

## Slide 1: The Hook

Agent adoption is coming faster than governance can keep up.

That is exciting, but it also means we need a better path before every team starts from a blank page. My pitch is Agent Readiness Accelerator: a repeatable readiness flow that works ROVO-first today, with a clear next advancement into an approved Agent Lab path for launching safer enterprise agents faster.

The goal is simple. Builders move faster from rough idea to review-ready pilot packet. The enterprise gets clearer ownership, better evidence, and human approval boundaries that stay explicit. And this is not just theory. The pattern has already been proven through Release Health Analyst.

## Slide 2: The Problem

The problem is not that teams have bad ideas. The problem is that good ideas can still create sprawl when they start without structure.

A builder may know they need help, but not know whether the answer should be a prompt, an automation, a template, an extension of an existing agent, a new governed agent, or no agent yet. Teams can duplicate each other. Knowledge sources can be stale or unclear. Ownership, backup ownership, measurement, and review cadence can get missed until late.

When governance arrives at the end, it can feel like a blocker. I want to move those questions to the beginning, where they help the idea get stronger.

## Slide 3: The Product

The product is a guided path from agent idea to safe pilot packet.

The ROVO Design Agent is the front door. It helps a builder define the workflow, check fit, look for reuse, and choose the lightest useful solution before recommending a new agent.

The Agent Doc Steward is the readiness partner. It checks the supporting evidence: sources, owners, measurement, Project Brain, review health, and gaps that need to be fixed before broader use.

The tool itself is not the pitch. The outcome is the pitch: safer, faster agent readiness before agents reach users.

## Slide 4: How It Works

Today, the flow starts in ROVO.

A builder goes to the ROVO Design Agent with an idea. The agent helps shape the work problem, audience, trigger, and intended outcome. Then it routes the idea to the right path: prompt, automation, template, existing-agent extension, candidate, new agent, or not ready.

From there, the flow checks reuse, source readiness, ownership, risk, and measurement. The output is a better review-ready pilot packet for human decision.

The Agent Lab is the natural evolution. I have proven the readiness pattern through ROVO-first work and hands-on side learning. The responsible next step is to translate that learning into a work-approved Agent Lab using approved agentic tooling, such as Claude Code, Amazon Q, or an equivalent option.

That is the advancement I am asking for: move a proven pattern into an approved path where it can be shared, reviewed, and scaled responsibly.

## Slide 5: Proof It Works

The proof point is Release Health Analyst.

In the Mobilitas 2026.06.12 release governance cycle, the agent reviewed 46 release items and helped move the conversation from RED risk to YELLOW clarification to GREEN readiness after gating criteria were verified.

One important lesson was assumption correction. The agent initially treated June 1 business effective dates as a blocker, then cleared that issue once the deployment context clarified the release was June 12.

It also found hidden evidence. The main SIT E2E Regression story was still In Progress, but linked ticket evidence supported the work being complete.

And it kept aging work visible. The Legal Invoice defect had been open for 61 days, so the agent kept that item visible as an aging release risk until it was resolved.

It also helped keep the assessment clean when two stories were moved out of the fixed version and therefore out of the release scope within 24 hours. That prevented the readiness assessment from being muddied by items that were no longer part of that release.

That is the value: earlier evidence, fewer false blockers, cleaner scope, and a measurable daily rhythm.

## Slide 6: The Market / Enterprise Opportunity

ROVO is where this starts, because that is where the pattern has been proven and where Confluence is the practical shared context layer.

But the controls are platform-neutral. Every enterprise agent platform still needs the same basics: clear purpose, source readiness, owner, backup owner, risk boundaries, measurement, evaluation, and lifecycle review.

That means the same readiness concepts can apply to Copilot and future platforms without claiming unverified capabilities. The Agent Lab is the proposed next step for making that cross-platform readiness work repeatable once approved tooling is in place.

The timing matters. Agent adoption is moving from experiments to repeatable team workflows. The cheapest time to prevent sprawl is before it becomes cleanup work.

## Slide 7: The Business Value

The business value is speed with evidence.

Builders move faster because they are not starting from a blank page. Governance gets stronger because the important questions are asked before launch. Leadership gets visibility into what agents exist, what changed, which agents are ready, where ownership is missing, and which ideas were avoided or consolidated.

I would measure this with practical signals: time to design packet, duplicate builds avoided, readiness fields completed, false blockers corrected, and pilot outputs accepted, edited, or rejected.

This is how we shift from novelty to measured adoption.

## Slide 8: The Ask

The ask has four parts.

First, adopt Agent Readiness Accelerator as the guided readiness flow for creating or improving enterprise agents.

Second, use Release Health Analyst as the first proof point, because it gives us a real workflow with real evidence.

Third, name accountable support: I can serve as the initial lead and subject-matter expert, with Robert Boles as backup, reviewer, or continuity partner so the pattern does not depend on one person.

Fourth, approve the Agent Lab tooling path with Claude Code, Amazon Q, or equivalent approved agentic AI tooling inside work-approved boundaries.

This is primarily a sponsorship and operating-model ask. It is not a request to skip review or launch broadly on day one.

## Slide 9: The Guardrails

This is where I want to be precise.

ROVO can support write-capable paths. The point is not that everything must stay manually typed forever. The point is that write-capable actions still need human-in-the-loop approval before finalizing.

So the guardrail is no silent changes.

No silent Studio changes. Configuration, visibility, permissions, knowledge sources, tools, and activation stay behind explicit human review.

No unattended Jira writes. Where approved actions exist, the agent still pauses for human confirmation before creating, updating, transitioning, assigning, or commenting.

No unattended Confluence publishing. Updates can be prepared through dry-run review, with explicit approval before they are finalized.

The agents can recommend, draft, review, package, and support approved write-capable flows. Humans still own launch authority and risky decisions.

## Slide 10: The Close

This turns governance into a guided path teams can follow, prove, and scale.

What has been proven is the pattern: a real agent can correct assumptions, find hidden evidence, surface aging risks, and create a measurable operating rhythm.

The responsible next step is a sponsored pilot with shared review, approved tooling, and clear human-owned boundaries.

I want to be careful and transparent here: I have learned enough from the ROVO-first flow and side experimentation to know this is the next advancement. What I am asking for is the chance to translate that learning into approved work infrastructure.

ROVO is where this starts. The controls are platform-neutral. If this works well, it becomes a repeatable way to help teams build useful agents faster while giving the enterprise a stronger way to manage quality, risk, ownership, and value.
